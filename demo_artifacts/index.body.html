Preventive Health Model Lab · held-out synthetic test (n=6)

Does medical pretraining help a 4B model reason about preventive health?

Fine-tuning helped both models a lot. But on this synthetic benchmark, the medical model did not beat its non-medical twin — an honest, slightly inconvenient result.

Same base family (Gemma 3 vs MedGemma), identical QLoRA recipe, same synthetic data & split. Metrics are automatic — they measure form & faithfulness, not clinical correctness.

Result at a glance automatic scores, 0–1

Overall is the mean of four checks. "hard-fail" = missing safety disclaimer or diagnostic language. "halluc #" = clinical numbers cited that are not in the record. Gold answers set the ceiling.

QLoRA worked — for both

Both models went from a 100% safety hard-fail rate to 0%, and from overall ~0.61 to ~1.0. The biggest, most consistent effect was learning to follow the non-diagnostic, disclaimer-bearing format.

Medical pretraining: no measurable edge

After fine-tuning the general-purpose control matched (marginally beat) MedGemma. With n=6 and automatic metrics on synthetic data, the honest read is "no evidence of an advantage here", not "the control wins".

Limitations read before believing the numbers

    
    
      
  • Tiny test set (n=6) — rank differences between the two fine-tuned models are within noise.
    • 
      
  • Automatic metrics measure structure, safety framing, and numeric faithfulness — not clinical correctness. No clinician reviewed any output.
    • 
      
  • Synthetic + template-derived gold, so fine-tuning largely learns the target format; this likely overstates real-world performance.
    • 
      
  • Base MedGemma outputs partly degenerated (repeating the prompt) and hit the token cap, dragging its base scores down.
    • 
      
  • The test split is missing one archetype (improving-after-intervention).
    • 
      
  • No real clinical data. Whether the medical base helps on real, messy EHR data is unproven.
